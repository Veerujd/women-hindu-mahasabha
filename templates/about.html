INTRODUCTION

About Veerashaiva Mahasabha

The Lingayat people are known for wearing a linga, an object representing a god; in this case, Shiva. Lingayat are a numerous group in India, especially in the state of Karnataka.

This group formed in the 12th century by Basava Bagewadi (1134-1196), a philosopher and a social reformer. He taught against the caste system and its regulations. He envisioned a casteless society, though the resulting society eventually began drawing up lines of caste distinctions again, creating Lingayat own hierarchy of social status after Basava's death.

Basava consented to the marriage of a Brahmin woman and a man from an untouchable caste. This story reached the ears of King Bijjala who punished the couple by putting out Lingayat eyes and dragging them to Lingayat deaths behind an elephant. Basava saw this punishment as unacceptable and left Kayla. He moved on to Kudula Sangama where he died in 1167.

Where are Lingayat Located?

Lingayat are found primarily in Karnataka, where Lingayat comprise a major population. Lingayat are also found in significant numbers in Maharashtra and Andrah Pradesh.

What are Lingayat Lives Like?

The Lingayat are vegetarian and refrain from alcoholic drinks. Lingayat are known to be a progressive people and there are people from many walks of life and religious expression to be found among them. Most work in government service, but Lingayat are from many occupations, including artisans, cultivators and priests.

What are Lingayat Beliefs?

Lingayat have a series of initiatory rites, including Lingadharane, the bestowing of the linga. This rite is usually performed within the first week of a child's life. From this day forward, the person will wear the stone as an act of worship of the god (Shiva), in a box around Lingayat neck. The linga is not seen as a mere representation of god, but rather god himself.

Lingayat adore Shiva, but Basava taught that his worshipers should worship him alone rather than numerous gods. Lingayats reject polytheism and the Vedas and follow the teachings of the Vachana sahitya instead.

LEGACY FOUNDER

Poojya Hanagalla Shri Kumara Swamigalu

Founder of Mahasabha

Sri Kumara Swamigalu was born in 1867 in Joyisana Haralalli village, located in Ranibennur Taluk of Dharwad District, Karnataka. His childhood name was Halayya.

He was born into a financially disadvantaged joint family. Due to their economic hardship, two or three members of the household would regularly visit nearby villages to seek alms (Kanthi) for sustenance. On some occasions, Swamigalu himself also participated in this practice.

At one point, an individual advised him to discontinue begging and instead pursue education. This advice left a deep impression on him, and he resolved to study.

He completed his primary education up to the third standard in his native village. Thereafter, he moved to Kajjari village to continue his studies and later appeared for the matriculation examination. Although he was recognized as one of the brightest students in his school, he was unable to pass the matriculation exam.

This outcome can be viewed as a reflection of destiny. Had he succeeded, he might have fulfilled his ambition of becoming a teacher in his village. However, it appears that a different path was intended for him—one in which he would dedicate his life to the service of humanity.

Birth and Early Background

Sri Kumara Swamigalu, a distinguished philosopher of religion and society, was born in 1867 CE in Joyisana Haralalli village of Ranebennur Taluk, Dharwad District, Karnataka. According to the Shalivahana calendar (year 1789), he was born on Bhadrapada Shuddha Trayodashi, on a Wednesday, during an auspicious Brahmi Muhurta.

He was affectionately named Halayya in his childhood. He was later revered as Hanagal Uddharaka, reflecting his role in uplifting society.

Family and Childhood

Halayya was born into a devout and spiritually inclined family associated with the Salimatha tradition, linked to the lineage of Kottur Appayya. His father, Basavappayya, and mother, Nilamma, were deeply religious, virtuous, and devoted to worship, embodying a sattvic way of life.

Despite their strong spiritual values, the family lived in extreme poverty within a large joint family system. To sustain themselves, two or three members regularly traveled to nearby villages seeking alms (Kantibhiksha), which formed the primary means of livelihood.

At the age of six, Halayya began his education (Aksharabhyasa) under the guidance of his grandfather in Gavathi village. Alongside his studies, he would accompany elders in seeking alms.

Turning Point Toward Education

During one such occasion, an elderly man admonished young Halayya, questioning why he was spending his life begging instead of studying and becoming wise. The words deeply affected him. Overcome with emotion, he returned home in distress, reflecting on the harsh truth conveyed to him.

This incident marked a decisive turning point. Halayya resolved to abandon begging and dedicate himself to education.

Schooling and Academic Life

He continued his studies up to the third standard in Gavathi under his grandfather’s guidance. Later, he moved to Kajjari village, near Devaragudda, for further education. There, he studied under the renowned teacher Joglekar Mahadev, whose institution was widely respected.

Halayya stayed in Kajjari with the support of patrons such as Hirematha Racayya and earned a reputation as a brilliant student. Subsequently, he traveled on foot with his companion Shiva Gowda Asundi to Dharwad to appear for the matriculation examination.

Although recognized as an outstanding student, Halayya did not pass the examination. This outcome highlights the unpredictability of human destiny. Had he succeeded, he might have become a village school teacher after completing training college. However, fate had destined him for a higher purpose—service to humanity.

Continued Learning and Family Conflict

After the examination, Halayya returned to his native place and began studying scriptures under scholars such as Nijaguna and Basavappayya Shastri. He also started teaching in a local school in Gavathi.

Meanwhile, his mother, Nilamma, wished to arrange his marriage. Halayya, however, was disinclined toward worldly life. In a decisive moment, he respectfully declined marriage, even offering his earnings (three hundred rupees) to his mother. Despite her insistence, he chose renunciation and eventually left his home.

Spiritual Quest and Association with Saints

Driven by a deep desire for spiritual knowledge, Halayya traveled to Hubballi and stayed at the monastery of Siddharudha Swami. There, he engaged in daily rituals, spiritual practices, and discourses, sustaining himself through alms.

While at Hubballi, he sought answers to his spiritual doubts from Siddharudha Swami. Although he did not initially attain complete clarity, he continued his pursuit by visiting other saints, including Jadesiddha of Emmiganur, where some of his doubts were resolved.

He then resumed his studies and spiritual practices, frequently moving between places in search of deeper understanding.

Discipleship under Basavalinga Swamiji

During this period, Halayya encountered Basavalinga Shivayogigalu, a great yogi from Elandur, known for profound spiritual realization and yogic practice. Drawn by his wisdom and spiritual depth, Halayya sought to become his disciple.

Basavalinga Swamiji tested Halayya rigorously to assess his character and commitment. After successfully enduring these tests, Halayya was accepted as a disciple. The two lived together in seclusion, including periods of spiritual practice in caves, where Halayya received Kriyopadesha (spiritual initiation).

The guru was deeply pleased with Halayya’s devotion, discipline, and adherence to dharma.

Guru’s Passing and Later Wanderings

Halayya accompanied his guru on travels to various villages, including Annigeri in Dharwad district. During this time, Basavalinga Swamiji fell ill and eventually attained Lingaikya (spiritual union with the divine).

The loss of his guru deeply affected Halayya. Like a calf separated from its mother, he experienced profound grief and restlessness. Seeking solace and direction, he left the place and wandered in search of spiritual fulfillment.

Further Spiritual Pursuits

In his continued quest, Halayya traveled to the Sorab region and associated with Gurubasava Swamiji, a respected spiritual leader. He also received support from devotees such as Kendappa Gowda of Malagaddi and stayed in monasteries like Ulavi, where he continued his spiritual practices and growth.

Succession at Hanagal Math

Hanagal was home to a renowned and ancient monastery in Dharwad district, which had long been a center of spiritual excellence. Over time, many eminent Mahaswamijis, known for their deep austerity (tapas), strict adherence to dharma, and profound detachment (vairagya), had led the institution. Their exemplary lives greatly enhanced the prestige and sanctity of the Math, spreading its reputation far and wide.

Among its distinguished leaders was Fakir Swamiji, who played a significant role in restoring and elevating the glory of the monastery. Through his pure character and spiritual accomplishments, he propagated the fame of the Math across different regions.

During this time, devoted followers from Ulavi, including Basavayya and Kendappa Gowda, were closely associated with Fakir Swamiji. As Fakir Swamiji advanced in age, he began searching for a capable successor to carry forward the responsibilities of the Math.

Kendappa Gowda and Basavayya persuaded Halayya (later **Sri Kumara Swamigalu**) to seek the darshan of Fakir Swamiji. When Halayya met him, Fakir Swamiji recognized his spiritual potential and proposed that he assume leadership of the Math.

However, Halayya initially declined with humility. He expressed that he had resolved to dedicate himself fully to the service of society and feared that administrative responsibilities and institutional obligations might hinder his mission. He requested the blessings of the Swamiji for the successful fulfillment of his broader social and spiritual goals.

Fakir Swamiji, pleased by Halayya’s sincerity and noble intent, responded that no true social service would ever be obstructed by such responsibility. On the contrary, he affirmed that only a worthy and selfless individual like Halayya could truly uphold and expand the mission of the Math.

After repeated requests and persuasion from the Swamiji and devotees, Halayya finally agreed to accept the responsibility. He was formally entrusted with the leadership of the Hanagal Math and began administering it with great dedication.

Leadership and Contributions

As the head of the Math, Sri Kumara Swamigalu carried out Dasoha (service) in an exemplary manner. He actively engaged in guiding devotees, spreading moral awareness, and imparting spiritual teachings to the public.

During this period, Fakir Swamiji, due to advanced age and illness, eventually attained Lingaikya (spiritual union with the divine). Prior to his passing, he formally designated Halayya as his successor and entrusted him with the full responsibility of the Math.

Halayya was given the monastic name Sadashiva Swamiji, and from then onward, he came to be widely known as Sri Kumara Swamigalu, the revered head of Hanagal Math.

Expansion of Religious and Educational Work

Under his leadership, the Math expanded its activities significantly. Sri Kumara Swamigalu undertook extensive travels across various regions to promote religious education and spiritual awareness.

Recognizing that the vast treasury of Indian religious knowledge and cultural heritage was preserved largely in Sanskrit, he took active steps to make such knowledge accessible. To achieve this, he established educational institutions (pathashalas) in several places, including: Haveri, Hubballi, Bagalkot, Abbigeri, Rona, and Halakeri.

He ensured the sustainability of these institutions by arranging permanent financial support and resources, thereby enabling continuous learning and dissemination of knowledge.

Establishment of the Philosophers’ Convention

In 1903, at Dharwad, a significant meeting was convened in which prominent leaders and scholars participated in deliberations. During this gathering, a resolution was passed to establish an All-India organization of philosophers.

Subsequently, in May 1904, under the presidency of Danasura Sirasangi Lingaraj Desai, the first Mahadhiveshana (grand convention) of the All India Philosophers’ Association was held. This convention aimed to address the stagnation in society by promoting awareness, intellectual awakening, and social upliftment through philosophical thought and practical engagement.

A major driving force behind these efforts, though often working behind the scenes, was Sri Kumara Swamigalu of Hanagal. His vision and leadership played a crucial role in shaping the movement and guiding its activities toward societal progress.

Promotion of Education and Scriptural Studies

Sri Kumara Swamigalu strongly believed in providing educational opportunities to society as a means of fostering religious awareness and cultural understanding. He initiated efforts to research, compile, and publish important works related to religion and philosophy, making them accessible to a wider audience.

To achieve this, he brought together teams of scholars from various regions, including: Thanjavur, Chennai, Karur, and Madurai. These scholars were engaged in the study of religious and cultural texts across multiple languages, including Sanskrit, Tamil, Telugu, Malayalam, and even Nepali. Efforts were made to translate these works into more widely accessible forms, thereby broadening their reach and impact.

Establishment of the Sivayoga Institution

Recognizing the need for a structured religious institution for the Veerashaiva community, Sri Kumara Swamigalu, guided by the suggestions of the ascetic Mallanarya, took decisive steps toward its establishment.

In 1908, at Bagalkot, during the fourth session of the Philosophers’ Conference, a resolution was passed to establish a religious institution known as Sivayoga Mandira. The aim of this institution was to promote religious knowledge, ritual awareness, and spiritual discipline, while also encouraging social reform and community development.

After careful consideration, a suitable location was chosen in a spiritually conducive environment near Mahakuta, on the banks of the Malaprabha River, in the Badami taluk. With the support of many scholars and devotees, the Sivayoga Mandira was formally established in 1912.

To ensure the institution’s financial sustainability, a ginning and pressing factory was established at Bagalkot. The income generated from this enterprise was used to support the activities and functioning of the institution.

Transition to a Social Organization: Dharmottejaka Movement

The All India Philosophers’ Association gradually evolved into a broader social and religious movement. In 1909, at Bankapur, the first Dharmottejaka conference was held, marking a significant transition toward organized social reform.

This event was presided over by Jagadguru Gangadhar Swamiji of Hubballi and witnessed the participation of approximately three thousand heads of various monasteries (mathadhipatis).

Under the guidance of Sri Kumara Swamigalu, the movement expanded its focus to include public welfare initiatives. Special emphasis was placed on: Medical research and healthcare, Providing affordable treatment to the poor, Training individuals in Ayurveda, and Promoting practical knowledge and self-reliance. Additionally, institutions such as Goshalas (cow shelters) were established, and further development of the Sivayoga centers was undertaken to serve both spiritual and social needs.

Final Years and Legacy

Sri Kumara Swamigalu dedicated his entire life to spiritual service, social upliftment, and the dissemination of knowledge. He worked tirelessly for the betterment of society, leaving behind a profound and lasting legacy.

After a life of unwavering service spanning sixty-three years, he attained Lingaikya (spiritual union with the divine) in 1929, on the Saptami of Krishna Paksha in the month of Magha, according to the Shalivahana calendar.

OUR VALUES & VISION

Vision & Mission

Charting our journey toward spiritual and community excellence.

Our Vision Statement

  • Promote Heritage: Encourage and support research on Veerashaiva ancient traditions as potential solutions to societal challenges.
  • Combat Social Issues: Develop innovative strategies to tackle modern social resistance, using cultural principles as a key tool.
  • Foster Collaboration: Create a platform for collaboration among leaders, policymakers, and community stakeholders.
  • Disseminate Knowledge: Share knowledge and findings through conferences, publications, and educational programs.
  • Policy Advocacy: Advocate for policies and regulations that support community development.

Our Core Mission

  • Global Leadership: Become a leading global organization in preserving cultural heritage.
  • Sustainable Solutions: Promote sustainable and effective social frameworks to reduce the burden on community resources.
  • Innovation and Development: Drive innovation in educational support and preventative community care measures.
  • Educational Impact: Enhance the global community’s understanding of our heritage and its role in modern development.
  • Collaborative Network: Build a robust international network working together to combat challenges through shared knowledge.
